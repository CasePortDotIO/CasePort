## What most people do

They make `/markets` a boring page that says:

* we serve Maryland
* we serve Texas
* we serve Florida

That is weak.

It does not sell.
It does not qualify.
It does not route.
It does not rank deeply.
It does not feel premium.

## What you should do instead

Treat `/markets` as a **hub page with three jobs**:

### 1. Buyer qualification

A law firm should land there and instantly understand:

* where CasePort currently has coverage
* which markets are active
* which markets are expanding
* where private access is available
* whether their geography is worth applying for now

This helps self-close serious firms because they can pre-qualify themselves.

### 2. Claimant routing

An injured person should be able to find the right local path fast:

* choose state
* choose city
* choose case type if needed
* enter the correct intake flow

That reduces confusion and increases match quality.

### 3. SEO and GEO scale foundation

This page should act as the parent for:

* `/markets/maryland`
* `/markets/texas`
* `/markets/florida`
* `/markets/baltimore`
* `/markets/houston`

Later, those pages can support deeper pages like:

* `/locations/baltimore/car-accidents`
* `/locations/houston/truck-accidents`

That is how you compound search, AI retrieval, and geo relevance over time.

# The top 1% architecture

Do it in layers.

## Layer 1: `/markets` as the command center

This page should include:

### Hero

Headline like:
**Explore Active and Expanding CasePort Markets**

Subhead:
**See where CasePort currently supports law firm growth and claimant routing, with additional markets opening over time.**

CTA:

* **Request Private Access**
* **Find Your Market**

### Market status blocks

Each market should have a status:

* Active
* Expanding
* Priority Waitlist
* Not Yet Open

This is powerful because it creates:

* clarity
* scarcity
* perceived operational discipline

Not every market should look equally available.

### Dual-path routing

Split the page clearly:

* **For Law Firms**
* **For Injured People**

For law firms:

* check whether your target market is covered
* see current availability
* apply for private access

For injured visitors:

* select your state or city
* start free case review

That is self-closing behavior. The page routes visitors without human intervention.

## Layer 2: state pages

Build:

* `/markets/maryland`
* `/markets/texas`
* `/markets/florida`

These pages should answer:

* is CasePort active here?
* what kinds of cases are supported?
* which cities are strongest?
* how do firms request access in this market?
* how do injured people start?

Each state page should link downward into city pages.

## Layer 3: city pages

Build:

* `/markets/baltimore`
* `/markets/houston`

These are higher-intent pages.

For buyers, they signal:

* local availability
* local case flow potential
* local market seriousness

For claimants, they signal:

* relevance
* proximity
* trust

## Layer 4: city + case-type pages

Later, your real scale pages become:

* `/locations/baltimore/car-accidents`
* `/locations/houston/personal-injury`
* `/locations/atlanta/truck-accidents`

This is where the self-closing ecosystem gets dangerous in a good way.

Why?

Because a person or law firm can land on a page that already matches:

* their geography
* their intent
* their problem
* their next step

That is how pages start pre-selling before anyone talks to them.

# The smartest sequencing

Do **not** build dozens of geo pages immediately.

That is not top 1% thinking.
That is premature sprawl.

Do it like this:

## Phase 1

Build only:

* `/markets`
* 3 strongest state pages
* 2 strongest city pages

Example:

* `/markets`
* `/markets/maryland`
* `/markets/texas`
* `/markets/florida`
* `/markets/baltimore`
* `/markets/houston`

Why this is smart:

* enough structure to look serious
* enough pages to establish hierarchy
* not so many that quality drops
* creates future template for scale

## Phase 2

Add only pages where one of these is true:

* you already have claimant demand
* you already have buyer interest
* the city has strong PI economics
* the city has meaningful search demand
* the city fits your expansion roadmap

This is elite behavior.
You expand where economics justify it.
